## 1. Purpose

This policy defines what constitutes malpractice, maladministration, plagiarism, and academic misconduct at ACLAS Global International Education Group. It sets out the procedure for investigating suspected cases, the standard of proof applied, the range of sanctions available, and the consequences for those found to have committed an offence. The Centre is committed to protecting the integrity of OTHM qualifications and the value of the certificates it issues, in line with OTHM's Centre Approval Criteria and the OTHM Guidance Statement to Centres on the Risk of AI.

## 2. Scope

This policy applies to:
- All learners enrolled on OTHM qualifications, regardless of mode of study
- All staff (employees, contractors, and volunteers) involved in the delivery, assessment, or quality assurance of OTHM qualifications
- All assessment evidence submitted for OTHM qualifications
- All Centre activities related to OTHM qualifications

## 3. Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Malpractice** | Any act or attempted act by a learner or staff member that compromises, or could compromise, the assessment process or the integrity of a qualification |
| **Plagiarism** | Presenting someone else's words, ideas, data, images, or creative work as one's own without proper acknowledgment. This includes copying from published sources, other learners' work, internet sources, or AI-generated content without attribution |
| **Collusion** | Working with one or more other people to produce work that is then submitted as individual work, unless collaboration is explicitly permitted |
| **Contract Cheating** | Paying, instructing, or otherwise engaging a third party (including essay mills, freelancers, friends, or family members) to complete assessment work on a learner's behalf |
| **Impersonation** | Having someone else complete an assessment or examination in place of the registered learner |
| **Fabrication** | Inventing or falsifying data, sources, evidence, quotations, references, or experimental results |
| **Misuse of AI** | Using artificial intelligence tools to generate substantive assessment content and presenting it as the learner's own original work |
| **Maladministration** | Failure by staff to follow OTHM or Centre procedures, including persistent errors in record-keeping, failure to register learners on time, or failure to cooperate with quality assurance activities |
| **Self-Plagiarism** | Reusing one's own previously submitted work (from the same or a different qualification) without acknowledgment or authorisation |
| **Standard of Proof** | The civil standard of 'balance of probabilities' — is it more likely than not that malpractice occurred? |

## 4. Use of Artificial Intelligence (AI)

In line with the OTHM Guidance Statement to Centres on the Risk of AI:

### 4.1 Permitted Use
Learners may use AI tools for:
- Research assistance and literature searching
- Brainstorming and idea generation
- Language editing, grammar checking, and proofreading
- Summarising source materials

### 4.2 Conditions
Where AI tools are used as permitted above:
- The use must be explicitly acknowledged in the submission
- The learner must state which tool was used and how
- The learner retains full responsibility for the accuracy and integrity of the submitted work

### 4.3 Prohibited Use
Learners must NOT:
- Use AI to generate complete or substantive assessment responses and submit them as their own work
- Use AI to paraphrase plagiarised content to evade detection
- Use AI to generate references or citations that do not exist

### 4.4 Detection
- All written submissions are processed through plagiarism-detection software (integrated with Moodle) which includes AI-content detection features
- Assessors and IQAs are trained to identify linguistic and structural indicators of unauthorised AI use
- The Centre reserves the right to conduct a viva voce (oral examination) with any learner where AI misuse is suspected

## 5. Prevention

The Centre takes the following steps to prevent malpractice:

- All learners receive guidance on academic integrity, referencing, and plagiarism during induction.
- All learners acknowledge in writing that they have read and understood this policy.
- Assignment briefs are designed to require application and reflection, reducing the potential for generic or AI-generated responses.
- Assessors and IQAs receive training on detecting malpractice, including the use of plagiarism-detection tools.
- Standardisation meetings include discussion of borderline malpractice cases to ensure consistent handling.

## 6. Reporting Suspected Malpractice

Anyone who suspects malpractice or plagiarism must report it to the Centre Quality Lead (quality@aclas.global). Reports may be made in person, by email, or anonymously. The Centre protects anyone who reports in good faith from retaliation.

## 7. Investigation Procedure

1. **Initial assessment** — The Centre Quality Lead reviews the allegation within 5 working days to determine whether a full investigation is warranted and the appropriate classification (minor, moderate, serious).
2. **Notification** — If an investigation proceeds, the respondent is notified in writing within 5 working days. The notification includes the nature of the allegation, the policy sections allegedly breached, the process and timeline, and the right to be accompanied by a supporter (not a legal representative).
3. **Investigation** — An impartial Investigating Officer is appointed (not the person who reported the allegation). The investigation may include:
   - Review of the assessment evidence, originality reports, and submission metadata
   - Interview with the respondent (documented; respondent may be accompanied)
   - Interview with the Assessor, IQA, or any witnesses
   - Review of Moodle access logs and IP records
   - Comparison with other work by the same learner
4. **Standard of Proof** — The Centre applies the civil standard: **balance of probabilities**. Is it more likely than not that malpractice occurred?
5. **Report** — The Investigating Officer produces a written report within 15 working days, setting out findings and recommending any sanctions.
6. **Decision** — The Centre Director makes the final decision on sanctions, informed by the report, within 5 working days.
7. **Notification** — The respondent is notified in writing of the decision, the reasons, any sanctions (with effect dates), and the right to appeal through the Appeals Policy within 10 working days.

## 8. Consequences

### For Learners

| Offence Level | Examples | Possible Sanction |
|:---:|---|------|
| **Minor / First Offence** | Poor referencing; minor unattributed paraphrasing; first instance of inadequate AI acknowledgment | Written warning; mandatory completion of referencing/academic integrity module; resubmission of the work with corrections |
| **Moderate** | Significant unattributed copying; first instance of collusion on individual work; unauthorised AI use for part of a submission | Disqualification from the unit assessment; learner required to complete a new, different assessment task; formal warning recorded on learner file |
| **Serious** | Contract cheating; impersonation; second serious offence; fabrication of data | Disqualification from the unit (no resubmission); suspension from the programme; notification to OTHM |
| **Very Serious** | Widespread or systematic contract cheating; impersonation in a live assessment; third or subsequent serious offence; falsification of entry/RPL documents | Permanent exclusion from the Centre; disqualification from the qualification; notification to OTHM |

### For Staff

| Offence Level | Examples | Possible Sanction |
|:---:|---|------|
| **Minor Procedural** | Isolated failure to follow assessment/IQA procedure; late reporting | Written warning; retraining; increased IQA monitoring |
| **Moderate** | Repeated procedural failures; providing inappropriate assistance to a learner | Written warning; removal from assessment duties for a period; mandatory retraining |
| **Serious** | Falsification of assessment records; deliberate assistance with malpractice; destroying/altering learner work; repeated serious misconduct | Termination of contract; notification to OTHM and relevant professional/regulatory bodies |

### Aggravating and Mitigating Factors

**Aggravating factors** (may increase severity): Premeditation or planning; attempt to conceal the malpractice; impact on other learners; previous findings of malpractice.

**Mitigating factors** (may reduce severity): Early and full admission; cooperation with the investigation; evidence of genuine misunderstanding rather than deliberate deception; first offence; personal circumstances that contributed to the behaviour.

## 9. Notification to OTHM

The Centre notifies OTHM of any confirmed case of malpractice or maladministration that could affect learner results or the integrity of qualifications, in accordance with OTHM's reporting requirements.

## 10. Appeals

A learner may appeal a malpractice decision through the Appeals Policy. A staff member may appeal through the Centre's internal grievance procedure.

## 11. Record Keeping

All investigation records are retained for a minimum of 3 years after the conclusion of the matter (including any appeals). Records include the initial allegation/report, all evidence gathered, interview notes, the investigation report, the decision and notification of outcome, and records of any appeal.

## 12. Monitoring and Reporting

- The Centre Quality Lead maintains a **Malpractice Register** recording all cases, outcomes, and sanctions.
- The Malpractice Register is reviewed **annually** to identify patterns (e.g., recurring issues with specific units, assessment methods, or cohorts).
- An anonymised summary is included in the annual Self-Assessment Report.
